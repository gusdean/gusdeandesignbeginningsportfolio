Course Goals


1. Consider the role of rhetoric, technology, and ethics in acts of making.

Rhetoric and technology greatly affect the acts of making. Rhetoric, for example, can have a profound effect on your designs, especially in the field of advertising. Sometimes the only reason someone buys a product or engages in a design is because of the way it is phrased or written, whether this engagement is a conscious or subconscious choice. Rhetoric can also make or break your relationship with design. I would not buy something that is advertised with a spelling mistake in the ad, or something that is not worded effectively. It would make me instantly not consider it. If that was off, then what else could be? Technology, on the other hand, can help with creating things. Many artists use a mix of mediums, while others are entirely analog or digital. For example, my father is a graphic designer, and started out in the 80s and 90s when he did not have access to a computer to do everything for him. He uses a computer mostly, as you have to as a graphic designer in the digital age, but also will hand-sketch designs, take photos, and manipulate text by hand to create a desired effect. Technology has made the act of making a little more democratic. Designing things is no longer this mystified practice. It still is in many ways, but if you need to design something, there are many programs (such as Canva) that will give you a workspace with useful templates. Anyone using these services can make something well-designed in a relatively short amount of time. It takes a while to get the hang of programs like the Creative Suite, but after practicing it can become second nature and allows designers to create things that would not be possible without the help of technology and design software. AI is speeding up this process, and some fear that this might make a graphic designer obsolete, but humans like things made by other humans, so I do not believe that this will ever put designers out of business. I could see smaller companies using AI art for quick campaigns or designs when needed, but there will always be a desire for human interaction and artistry. Ethically speaking, it is difficult, because AI has been accused of stealing peoples’ designs and art styles. These AI image generators are fed thousands if not millions of images from the web indiscriminately, learn the style of each of those images, and can apply them to create something from a prompt. Artists unknowingly have their works processed by these learning models and their style can be replicated, which some, including myself, view as potentially being unethical. It can hurt individual creators when their style is no longer needed. In my freshman year of high school, I read a book by futurist Michio Kaku, where he talked about the “caveman principle.” This principle means that despite advances in technology, we still crave human interaction on a fundamental level, and for the majority of people this will trump any advances that make human interaction unnecessary. For example, I vastly prefer ordering food in a restaurant or drinking at a bar because I like the collective interaction component. I could very well order DoorDash and not speak to anyone, but I like the collective action of going to a restaurant and ordering to another person. Additionally, we are at a point in society where I could, hypothetically, not interact with anyone for the rest of my life. I could take online school, work from home, FaceTime my friends instead of hanging out with them in person and order my meals online. But almost no one lives like this. These conveniences are great, and I love being able to call my parents or my friends studying abroad in a different time zone, but I love human interaction too much to ever become a hermit. I do not believe that AI will completely make human design obsolete for this reason.

2. Leverage Design Principles.

I really like using the principles of design in my work. I like certain things and have a particular taste, but I love learning the “why” of why I am drawn to something. I think it is interesting, because everyone has a taste in design, whether they are aware of it or not. I think that people just are not used to assigning words to stuff that looks good, because “good” is subjective and hard to quantify. I think that symmetry, the golden ratio, color palettes, etc. show up in lots of designs because they are compelling, and I like learning about why I may think something is cool-looking. I do not think about this fully day-to-day; I have certain styles of design that I appreciate, and I think critically about some of them, but I don’t stop to think about the “why” behind their appeal. I think this class has helped me with recognizing my own taste and assigning words to previously intangible attributes of my favorite art and design work.

3. Engage with others in a workshop environment.

Collaboration is vital to producing a good design. In an incredibly selfish way, I like making things by myself because no one has any objections to my taste. I also appreciate working in group settings; you only see the world from your own perspective, and even practicing empathy will not fully put yourself in someone else’s shoes. Everyone has their own taste, and working together and meeting in the middle ends up producing good designs. Also, in the real world, many designers work in groups frequently. Unless you are doing freelance work and given significant creative freedom, you will have to work with a team of people if you work at an ad agency or a design firm. Collaboration is a vital and necessary skill to develop, especially before fully entering the professional world.



4. Practice revision within and across media forms.

Revision is a great practice that is sharpened in this class. In the past, I was very much a “one-and-done” kind of guy; in high school I would often submit papers without proofreading. I think that college, and this class, have taught me to slow down and iterate more in my design. In my architecture studio that I am taking this semester, we do not have set due dates outside of the final project except for desk critiques, where a professor or a teaching assistant will come around and critique your work during class time. This is vital for your success as a designer. If I just did my final project without any rough copies or half-finished work, it wouldn’t be fully formed. It is best to get your ideas out, however half-baked or crude they may be, then get feedback and reassess. This has also taught me the virtue of working slowly and not procrastinating, since I will achieve a better result.

5. Learn to present your work.

I have always been a nervous presenter and have struggled with anxiety, both in my daily life and in presentation settings. I have so far presented significantly more in college than in high school and luckily have become more comfortable with it. In almost any job you will need to present your work, so developing this skill now is crucial. I also think that presenting your work in a certain way is an aspect of design, as it is shaping how your work will appear to the audience. For this class and my studio class I have to present frequently, which I used to dread but now I appreciate.
